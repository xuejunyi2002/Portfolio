About the Project

Project Introduction

Nunavut Tales is a personal VR game prototype exploring how environmental storytelling and physical interaction can help players engage with the effects of permafrost thawing in northern communities.

The project combines a Unity based first person journey with a custom sensing boot that uses magnetic resistance to simulate the difficulty of moving across unstable ground. I designed the narrative, environments, UI, interaction concept, and physical prototype as one connected player experience.

Overview

Project Overview

Nunavut Tales began as an exploration of how an interactive experience could communicate an environmental issue through both narrative and embodied play.

The prototype places the player in a journey inspired by thawing permafrost. Rather than explaining the issue through text, the player moves through changing environments while physically experiencing resistance through a custom sensing boot.

Research

Research Before Design

The project was inspired by a CBC documentary about the effects of climate change and thawing permafrost on northern communities.

Before developing the experience, I visited the Inuit Gallery of Vancouver to better understand the relationship between Inuit art, land, and nature. I also spoke with Inuit artist Koomuatuk Curley from Kinngait, Nunavut, about changes affecting daily life, food practices, travel, hunting, sea ice, and safety.

The conversation helped me move beyond a general climate theme and think more carefully about how environmental change affects daily routines, movement, and a person's relationship with the land.

Narrative Design

From Village to "Safe Place"

The player begins in a northern village affected by unstable ground and travels south after hearing about a place believed to be safe.

The journey moves through five stages:

01  Beginning
02  Village
03  Snowy Forest
04  Highway
05  Final Safe Place

The final destination is a city — safer for the player, but also connected to the environmental systems driving climate change. The journey works as both a physical progression and a metaphor for displacement and environmental responsibility.

Environment Design

Building the Journey in Unity

I built the prototype environment in Unity around a sequence of snowy landscapes, village spaces, unstable terrain, camps, and travel routes.

Each location supported a stage of the journey and communicated isolation, changing terrain, and increasing movement difficulty.

  • Scene composition and environmental progression
  • Landmark placement and player navigation
  • Visual atmosphere and storytelling through space

UI Design

Supporting the Player Journey

I designed the UI to support the narrative experience without competing with the environment.

The interface included: start screen, loading screen, save selection, main gameplay HUD, and navigation and status information.

The visual direction used transparent layers, cool tones, and environmental imagery to maintain continuity between the menus and the game world.

Physical Computing

Designing the Sensing Boot

The boot uses magnetic components and an Arduino system. An iron plate beneath the player creates resistance at selected moments in the game.

The goal was not to recreate the physical condition literally — it was to add a bodily sensation that reinforced what was happening in the digital environment.

Interaction Goal

Create a physical change in movement that corresponds with environmental events in the game.

Technical Approach

Use an Arduino controlled magnetic system to activate resistance beneath the boot.

Player Experience

Connect visual instability in the Unity environment with a noticeable change in physical movement.

Game Design Reflection

What This Project Taught Me

01

Interaction Can Carry Meaning

A game mechanic can communicate more than a written explanation. Connecting movement resistance to the environment helped me understand how physical interaction can support narrative and emotional context.

02

Worlds Need a Clear Point of View

Environment design, story progression, UI, and interaction all need to support the same central idea. This project taught me to evaluate each design choice through the player experience rather than treating every element separately.

03

Research Changes Creative Decisions

The early research and conversation with an Inuit artist changed how I thought about the project. It reminded me that culturally connected work requires listening, context, and careful limits on what a prototype can claim to represent.
